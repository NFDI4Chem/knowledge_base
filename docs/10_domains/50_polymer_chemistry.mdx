## Introduction

Polymer chemistry is focused on the synthesis and characterization of monomer units, macromolecules and resulting materials for specific applications. Polymeric materials can be subdivided by their origin into synthetic, inorganic and biopolymers. The toolbox for monomer synthesis and modification, their polymerization as well as post-functionalization are often derived from organic chemistry, as well as many characterization methods, especially for monomers or small polymers. However, since macromolecules can feature highly complex structures, e.g. brushes, dendrimers, rings and star shapes, advanced methods such as (2D) SEC, LCCC, DOSY NMR, or electron microscopy need to be applied in order to obtain an exhaustive structure determination. For their subsequent application as high-tech material, their mechanical and physical properties play an important role as well, where cyclic voltammetry or rheological characterization are just a few examples for the vast array of techniques that are available.

Due to the complexity of macromolecular architectures, many different polymerization methods are available, each with their own advantages and disadvantages, which are briefly described in the following. Generally, the standards for monomer synthesis can be adapted from organic chemistry (link). However, each polymerization method has individual requirements that are described in the respective section.

Step growth polymerization

Monomers:
For step-growth polymerization many different functional groups can be used, depending on whether it is a polyaddition or polycondensation reaction. Most common groups are hydroxyl, carboxyl, amines, thiols, isocyanides.
Required information is: chemical structure from NMR and IR, molar mass from mass spectrometry, absorbance/fluorescence from UV/Vis.

Polymerization:

Materials required:
Monomers: bi-functional monomers for homo polymerization (e.g. hydroxyl- and carboxyl-groups), two or more bi-functional monomers with two different reactive groups (e.g. di-isocyanide and diamine) for AB-type copolymers.
catalyst depending on type of reaction and solubility (acids, bases, transition metals etc.)
optional materials:
solvents
emulsifiers
relevant information: bulk, solution or emulsion polymerization with respective concentrations of monomers, emulsifiers, catalysts and type of solvent.

Chain growth polymerization

Monomers:
A successful radical polymerization requires monomers with unsaturated carbon-carbon double bonds (C=C). Also, functional groups must be compatible with radicals (thiols, halogenides etc. have to be avoided or introduced in post-polymerization functionalization). Therefore, monomer synthesis has to be planned carefully. In controlled radical polymerization techniques, small molecule initiators can also be functionalized individually.
required information: chemical structure from NMR and IR, molar mass from mass spectroscopy, absorbance/fluorescence from UV/Vis.

Polymerization:
Materials required:
monomers: vinylic molecules such as methylmethacrylate, styrene, ethylene etc.
radical initiator (depending on solubility and mode of initiation): thermal initiator (azo- or peroxi-based initiators depending on temperature range), photo-initiator (depending on wavelength), redox-initiator etc. Common initiators are AIBN, VA055, BPO.
optional materials:
solvents: liquid monomers can be polymerized in bulk, but to avoid thermal runaway of the reaction often solvents are added, they also have to be stable towards radicals, elevated temperatures or light irradiation. Common solvents are DCM, toluene, dioxane, DMF, DMSO.
chain transfer agents: the resulting chain length of the polymer can be controlled with chain transfer agents to achieve short polymers and oligomers such as thiols and halogenides.
for controlled radical polymerization techniques additional reactants such as nitroxides (for NMP), transition metal complex (for ATRP) or RAFT-initiators may be required
for emulsion polymerization the type and amount of emulsifier
relevant information: bulk, solution or emulsion polymerization with respective concentrations of monomers, initiator, emulsifier, chain transfer agent and type of solvent.

Polymerization process:

The process is usually described in a free text including the information on materials (amount and type). Variation of parameters can be displayed in tables.
relevant information are reaction conditions such as temperature, wavelength, reaction vessel, inert atmosphere, stirring etc. Further observations such as precipitants, color change, viscosity change, solubility, yield.
Information on the purification such as precipitation in a non-solvent (what kind of solvent, how much, warm/cold, how often precipitated, redissolved in which solvent), dialysis (against which solvent, how long, how many times solvent changed, solvent gradients)

resulting polymer description:
appearance of the material: sticky/not sticky, solubility, color
chemical information: monomer composition and if possible end-groups from NMR, functionality from IR or Raman, absorbance or fluorescence from UV/Vis, molecular weight and dispersity from SEC or MS
physical properties: glass transition temperature and melting point from DSC, thermal decomposition from TGA
rheological properties: flexibility, toughness, elasticity

Post-polymerization functionalization:

Since radicals are highly reactive, many functional groups or biological materials are not compatible with these harsh reaction conditions. These moieties can be introduced in post-polymerization modifications.
here relevant information includes:
type and amount of functional group that is introduced (if separately synthesized NMR, IR, MS data), calculation of the ratios between polymer and functional groups.
Comparative polymer data before and after functionalization: change in molecular weight and dispersion from SEC or MS, change in chemical functionality from NMR, IR, Raman, UV/Vis, physical and mechanical changes from DSC, TGA, rheological measurements, change in color, solubility etc.

## ELNs and Other Tools

For effective data management, software tools should be selected in a uniform manner within a project or research group with the aim to organize and streamline workflows. This involves establishing clear usage guidelines, including metadata templates drawn from minimum information standards for a given method, where available. These should be outlined in a data management plan (DMP) for each project. NFDI4Chem provides an RDMO template specifically tailored to the needs of chemists.

An electronic lab notebooks (ELN) helps in the day-to-day planning and structured documentation of experiments, while some also assist in data workflow management. The use of an electronic lab notebook is strongly recommended to ensure the proper preservation of data and metadata in a FAIR manner For scientists not sure about the choice of the right ELN, the ELN-Finder (https://eln-finder.ulb.tu-darmstadt.de/) lists many options and the article on choosing an ELN provides further assistance.

## Publishing Data

For synthesis data and analytical data, specific repositories such as [Chemotion Repository](https://www.chemotion-repository.net/welcome) (synthesis data, analytical data, sample registration), [MassBankEU](https://massbank.eu/MassBank/) (mass spectra), or [nmrXiv](https://nmrxiv.org/) (for NMR data) are recommended. For other types of data the generic repository [RADAR4Chem](https://radar.products.fiz-karlsruhe.de/de/radarabout/radar4chem) is recommended.
Publishing research data is important to allow other researchers to reuse it. To reach as many researchers as possible, choosing the right repository can be crucial (check the [Choose a Repository](https://knowledgebase.nfdi4chem.de/knowledge_base/docs/choose_repository/) article for more information).
